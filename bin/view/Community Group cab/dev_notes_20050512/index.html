 Face to face
 Questions for Andy
 NetBSD


 Roles
 -
 Started with a small core team.  About 5 people.  Starter, self-selected, self-perpetuating board.  Significant turnover.  Largely
 due to burnout.  Arm-twisting to get people to sign-up.  In the last year or so, formality of structure.  A BOD that oversees non-technical aspects.  Distinct from core group.  Nominated and voted on by developers.  Developers have signed an agreement saying they won't submit encumbered code.  Nominated by another developer.  Core would accept or reject.  Then sign a contributor agreement.  Recently there is increased formality in the developer nomination process.  Get names out to the broader community sooner.  Nominators sponsor their folks. Connect folks with experts etc.  There is not a formal code review process.  Most go in quickly with normal testing.  Bigger changes.....???
 Development Process
 -
 There is not the same notion of FCS quality all the time.  General sense that ensuring that the trunk remains functional is a good thing.
 Strategy of roll-forward/recovery.  Some people who have a specialty – c-style to man page formating.  Developer commits a rough manpage and cleanup done by someone else.  A large volume of email – one list per port where a port is a particular machine that it runs on.  Other lists that are targeted at specific areas of the kernel.  Technical management by email blizzard.  Process is not all that different from what goes before the ARC except there is a not a clear....????.
 Q&A
 -
 Q:  Is there direction set by the core group?
 A:  There are a couple of big ideas that were set by core group – emphasis on extreme portability, avoid duplicating drivers between
 ports, security is important.  Most is set by example.
 Q: How early on does design review take place?
 A:  It's very flexible.  Sometimes people design on the fly on the list.  Propose a design, get feedback, come back with a new design.  No formal signed documents.  People working the projects keep informing the relevant lists of progress.
 Q: How is testing typically done by developer?
 A: There are test suites for some pieces.  No universal test suites. Focus on portability means that develop tests on multiple platforms, but nobody has all.  Easily cross-compilable.
 Q:  Drivers – modification to all drivers?
 A:  Depends on the nature of the change.  If you can accommodate the change with a mechincal edit, some people take on the task of ???
 Q: How successful do you think this is as an overall process?
 A: Depends on what the goals are.  It works largely because they are relatively selective on picking people to be developers.  It breaks down somewhat – not something I would want to use here at Sun.  We need somewhat more structure.  One piece we can work from, bootstrapping the governance structure is always going to be messy – choosing leadership.  Important to have clear term of service and avoid any kind of small click of insiders running the show.
 Q: How does someone who is new make a change?
 A: People send in bug reports with fixes in them.  Get sponsored as a developer to get their little project integrated without having someone act as middleman.
 Q: How big is the total community?
 A: Developers:  200, but not everyone is active.
 Q: Given their current process, could they expand beyond 300?
 A: They would need to get more formal.
 Q: What kind of characteristics do they value in developers?
 A: Strong technical strength and respect for the direction of the project.  Ability to work with existing developers.  Fair amount of
 strong personalities.

 Roles
 -
 Core Team, set of committers, people submit to committers, broken into specific areas:  kernel, ports outside, manpages.
 Core team operates as escalation path for disagreements.  Fairly loose in terms of what we would look at as a process.
 Changes to certain areas require at least one additional developer to review the code.  No matter what.  If you're a committer and you find someone else to review your code there is not much else you need to do.
 Releases
 --
 Branches out of source repository.  Release cycles.  Mode to stabilize for release, go to release.  2.5.1 to 2.6 model.
 NetBSD – trunk and ??? gets branched which gets stabilized.  New development continues on the trunk.
 Questions
 -
 Q: What happens if there is something that went into the pre-Beta trunk that needs to be back-ported? Not this one.
 A: Each release has a release engineering team that is responsible for pullup/backporting.  Amount of backporting depends on the size of the change.
 Linux

 See Keith's mail.
 Characteristics of developers
 -
 Desire to do early review.  Talking about abstract things is ignored, shouted down. Seems like there is a model of building a prototype, then someone else comes along to take it further – iterating in the gate is not at all unusual.  Need a willingness to develop speculatively.
 Breakage of the ports is a continuous phenomenon.  Tremendous willingness to accept new features that are not intrusive without regard to the cost of maintaining them.  Best efforts of the developer rather than guarantee.
 Code being removed is extremely unusual.  Rather they are marked broken.  Assumed that there is some value in the code for the next person that comes along.
 Questions
 -
 Q:  Is there a CBE – Common Build Environment?  How are compilers and tools rolled out?  What do you do in terms of changes to the tool chain?
 A: A lot of people have a large number of different compilers etc. Assumed that it will work with compilers that are available at the time
 of release.  That's x86.   Non-x86, declares tool chain that is golden.  If you don't use....
 A: NetBSD – a copy of gcc in the tree that is a gcc release plus critical patches.  For the most parts, established ports will compile
 with that version.  When new ports show up they may need to use.  ??? Self-contained as part of the distribution.  Like nightly -t.
 Q: Changes that require synchronous updates to multiple sub-systems?
 A: Covered
 If it affects x86, then it is expected that you are talking to the maintainers of the different subsystems to keep it all synchronized.
 Linus likes small changes.  Keep things separate.   Structure the changes so that they can be updated independently etc.
 Q: From an arch review perspective, how is binary compatibility treated?
 A: Linus has gone on record....but by no means absolute.  Stability of the sys calls is not absolute.  If it's important enough it will be made even in the....
 A: NetBSD – mostly reviewed from pullup from the trunk to release branches.  Everything is compiled in one go.  Focus is on kernel
 stability for release branches, not so much for the trunk.  You would not see a change to remove an option from a release branch without a very good reason...it might happen in the trunk.
 Q: Do the version numbers that NetBSD uses have the similar connotation that we use?
 A: No – much more of a fuzzy sense of how big the change is.
 Q: Why would someone choose Net vs Free BSD?
 A: It runs on my hardware.  I know the developer.  It has some feature I depend on that is not in the other.
 Q: Stability of commands – do they incrementally grow?  Adding and removing functionality.
 A: GNU stuff just has things added.  Rare to take it out.  UtilLinux is stagnant.  Glibc takes pains to ensure compatibility.  No consistent set of commands.
 Copyrights
 --
 NetBSD – there is a mix of copyrights in the tree.  For some, there is a NetBSD Foundation copyright – code by xxxx.
 Linux – aribitrarily rewrite the copyright if your changes is big enough....but there are no rules or guidelines.  If you believe that you
 have contributed something really meaningful, then you should add your copyright.
 Thoughts – may need a credit file.  Putback logs.  CVS history.  How do you make sure that credit is given to folks.  Might want to think about copyright analysis.  Who gets to arbitrate – perfect CAB issue.
